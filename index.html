Simplicity Matches Scale on Current Imitation Learning Benchmarks

Paper arXiv Code

BCAT (32M params, <10M trainable) and BCAT-Small (13.9M params, <2.2M trainable), simple deterministic behavioral cloning transformers, match or exceed billion-parameter VLAs on standard manipulation benchmarks at a fraction of the compute.

Abstract

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have established new frontiers in learning autonomous fine-grained manipulation policies, achieving state-of-the-art performance across several simulation benchmarks and physical hardware tasks. While real-world robotic setups are inherently unique and difficult to reproduce, simulation benchmarks offer deterministic, controllable environments that serve as ideal testbeds for systematic policy evaluation.

In this paper, we shed light on two underexplored aspects of current trends in imitation learning for robotic manipulation. First, we demonstrate that large-scale pre-trained VLA policies with billions of parameters can be matched on standard benchmarks by simple, nonautoregressive action transformers with fewer than 10 million trainable parameters. We show that a bidirectional decoderonly action transformer trained with Behavioral Cloning (BC) achieves competitive performance comparable to state-of-theart results on both the LIBERO and Meta-World simulation benchmarks, even with fewer than 3 million trainable parameters.

Second, we argue that these standard robotics benchmarks suffer from insufficient task complexity, lack multimodality in the action distribution, rarely require language instructions for task disambiguation, and are fundamentally small-data regimes. Under these conditions, we show that generative action modeling paradigms such as flow matching are not justified by the task demands. Flow-matching-based action diffusion transformers of comparable parameter count exhibit lower success rates and produce jerky, noisy trajectories. By contrast, a standard deterministic BC policy yields substantially higher success rates, even with vision-only input and no language conditioning.

Validation Loss is Not Rollout Performance

A key finding of our work is the disconnect between validation loss and downstream rollout success. Both BCAT and Flow MM-DiT reach their minimum validation loss around epoch 20 (red marker), yet rollout performance at this checkpoint is poor for both policies. The best rollout success rates emerge much later, around epoch 60 (green marker), well after the validation loss has plateaued or even begun to increase.

Crucially, BCAT's validation loss is consistently higher than Flow MM-DiT's throughout training, yet BCAT achieves dramatically better downstream rollout performance (93.2% vs. 65.9% average success on LIBERO). This confirms that validation loss is a misleading proxy for policy quality in imitation learning: a model that fits the offline data distribution better (lower val loss) does not necessarily induce a better occupancy measure at deployment. This is a direct consequence of the covariate shift problem and underscores the necessity of closed-loop evaluation.

Validation loss on LIBERO suites. Both models reach minimum val loss at epoch ~20 (red), but best rollout performance occurs at epoch ~60 (green). Despite having worse val loss, BCAT achieves far superior downstream success rates.

On the Redundancy of Language Conditioning

We train BCAT without the frozen BERT embedding layer, using vision-only input (9.2M parameters). Language only helps in 2 of 4 LIBERO suites (Goal, Long), while providing no benefit on Meta-World where each task is fully disambiguated by the initial visual observation. This demonstrates that current benchmarks do not require the language grounding that VLA architectures are designed for.

Removing language (blue) only degrades 2 of 4 LIBERO suites.

On Meta-World, language provides no benefit—vision alone fully disambiguates all 50 tasks.

How Small Can We Go?

BCAT-Small uses only 2.2M trainable parameters (13.9M total), replacing EfficientNet-B0 with MobileNetV4, reducing the decoder to 2 layers, and swapping BERT for MiniLM-L6-v2. Despite this drastic reduction, it achieves 93.8% average success on LIBERO—slightly exceeding the full 32M-parameter BCAT (93.2%). It even outperforms on 3 of 4 LIBERO suites (Object: 99.7 vs. 93.0, Goal: 94.3 vs. 91.0, Spatial: 97.3 vs. 94.7). The only settings where additional capacity helps are LIBERO-Long and Meta-World, which pose genuinely harder multi-step or multi-task learning problems.

This inverts the conventional scaling narrative: on current benchmarks, smaller models generalize better because the data regime is too small for larger models to leverage their capacity without overfitting.

Key Findings

1
The data regime is fundamentally small.
With 50 demonstrations per task, the training data covers a narrow region of the state-action space. Large overparameterized models tend to overfit to noise and ignore signal.
2
The task structure is too simple.
Each task is fully disambiguated by the initial visual observation alone. Language conditioning provides no measurable benefit on Meta-World and only helps on 2 of 4 LIBERO suites.
3
The action distributions are unimodal.
Expert demonstrations follow similar trajectories. There is no genuine multimodality that would necessitate generative models. Deterministic L1 regression consistently outperforms flow matching.

We urge the community to develop simulation environments with genuinely multimodal tasks, visually ambiguous scenarios requiring language disambiguation, larger demonstration datasets, and longer-horizon compositional tasks.
